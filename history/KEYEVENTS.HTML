1563 Sir William Cecil purchases Theobalds from John Elyott, initially intended as a quiet family home.
1564 Queen Elizabeth I visits for the first time. In preparation for the Queen's future visits, he begins to build a grand house to better accommodate her.
1598 Sir Robert Cecil dies and his son, Sir Robert Cecil, inherits the estate.
1603 Queen Elizabeth I dies. King James I stays at Theobalds for a few nights while on his way to London, and immediately takes a liking to the estate.
1607 Robert Cecil trades Hatfield House with James I in exchange for Theobalds. The King orders the gardener, Munten Jennings, to expand the property.
1620 By this time, the palace includes a deer park at least 2500 acres in size. The King orders a brick wall (9½ miles long) to surround the estate.
1625 King James I dies at Theobalds Palace and is succeeded by his son, Charles I, who is pronounced King at the gates.
1649 After Charles I is executed, the palace is passed to Parliament.
1650 Oliver Cromwell's Parliament carries out a survey and concludes that Theobalds Palace is in excellent condition. Despite this, the buildings are demolished and approximately £8275 (over £1.2 million today) is distributed among the troops serving in the English Civil War.
1762 The palace is sold to George Prescott. After a year, he begins constructing a mansion on the former deer park, known as Theobalds Park.
1783 A survey says 'The former Palace of Theobalds [is] now in a ruinous condition'.
c. 1820 The Meux family begin renting Theobalds Park and later purchase the estate.
1919 Admiral Sir Hedworth Meux signs a covenant bestowing The Cedars to Cheshunt Urban District Council for the enjoyment of the people of Cheshunt.
1921 Sir Hedworth Meux and the Earl of Cavan officially open The Cedars Park to the public.
